## The One Thing That Matters Most

**Conviction sells. Technique closes.**

If you believe your offer will help them, you will say it right — tone, pace, confidence — without thinking about it. If you don't believe it, no script fixes that.

Before any sales call: remind yourself of someone whose life got better because of you.

---

## Tonality — How You Say It Is the Sale

90% of persuasion is delivery. Not the words. The words are the last 10%.

If you sound uncertain, no script saves you. If you sound certain, you barely need one.

**The frame to carry into every call:** You've done this before. You know what they need. You're not hoping this works — you already know it does. Talk like that.

**Three tones to rotate:**

| Tone | Sounds like | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| **Certainty** | Calm, unhurried, no uptalk | When you make a claim or name the price |
| **Curiosity** | Genuine, leaning in | During the problem-finding phase |
| **Warmth** | Like you're talking to a friend | After they open up about the real problem |

**The two things that kill your tonality:**
- Speeding up when you're nervous — slow down instead. Pausing sounds confident. Rushing sounds scared.
- Ending statements like questions (uptalk) — "So this would help you... save time?" sounds like you're asking for permission. Drop the tone at the end.

## The CLOSER Framework — Full Call Structure

| Step | What you do |
|---|---|
| **C — Clarify** | Why are they here? What's the goal? |
| **L — Label** | Restate their problem back. Get them to say "yes, that's it." |
| **O — Overview** | What have they tried before? Run the pain cycle: *what'd you try, how long, how'd it go, what else?* |
| **S — Sell the vacation** | 3 short stories that explain why your thing solves it. No features. Sell the outcome. |
| **E — Explain away concerns** | Handle objections before and after you name the price |
| **R — Reinforce** | After they say yes — confirm, welcome them, make them feel good about the decision |

**Only 3 things ever come out of your mouth on a call:**
1. Questions
2. Restatements (their words back to them)
3. Short stories (to break a belief)

## The Three Tens — Why They Didn't Close

When a call doesn't close, one of three things was low. Figure out which one.

1. **Your offer** — do they believe it actually solves their problem?
2. **You** — do they trust you personally to deliver?
3. **The company behind you** — do they trust Zentara as a real, legitimate operation?

Most people assume it's always #1 and go back to pitching harder. It's usually #2 or #3.

**Use this after every call that doesn't close.** Ask yourself: which ten was lowest? That's what you address in the follow-up — not the price, not the features.

## Implication Questions — Make the Pain Feel Real Before You Pitch

The CLOSER "Overview" step runs the pain cycle. This makes it sharper.

After they describe the problem, don't pitch yet. Ask what the problem *costs* them.

**The sequence:**
1. "How long has this been going on?"
2. "What has that cost you — in time, stress, money?"
3. "What happens if it's still the same a year from now?"

You're not asking to be mean. You're helping them feel where they're stuck *before* you show them the exit. The Sell the Vacation lands harder when they're already desperate to leave.

Most people pitch too early. This is the step that makes them sell themselves.

## Sell the Vacation, Not the Plane Flight

They don't want to hear about your process, your tools, your sessions, your system.

They want to hear what their life looks like after.

> "I'll stop spending 3 hours a day doing this manually"
> "I'll finally have something that works while I'm not watching it"

Lead with the destination. The how is just the route.

## The 5 Objections — What to Say

### TIME — "It's not a good time / I'm too busy"

- **Busy season:** "If you learn this when you're busy, it'll stick. The time you need the most support is when things are already full."
- **No time in my day:** "You're not adding work — you're cutting the stuff that's eating your time. That's what I help you figure out."
- **When/then fallacy:** "I'll do it when things settle down" = they need the thing to get the thing. Call it out warmly: *"That's like saying I'll go to the doctor once I feel better."*

---

### MONEY — "I can't afford it / too expensive"

- "If this adds even a few hours back to your week permanently — is it worth it?"
- "You're going to pay for this either way. In money now, or in time over the next year. Which do you prefer?"
- "You've found money for rent, taxes, unexpected bills. This is a choice, not a limit."

---

### FIT — "I'm not sure this is for me"

- "What you've been doing has been getting what you've been getting. Something has to change."
- "If this were exactly what you needed — would you do it?" → If yes: "So what's the gap between that and this?"
- Pain of staying vs. pain of changing: *"Which hurts more — staying here, or going through the adjustment?"*

---

### AUTHORITY — "I need to talk to my partner / spouse"

- "What do you think they'd say no to?" — overcome *that*, not the imaginary person
- "Does your partner approve of the current situation? No? Then why would they be against something that fixes the thing they already don't approve of?"
- **The real issue:** They're asking for permission when they need support. "You don't need their permission to invest in yourself. You just need to tell them — not ask."

---

### STALL — "I need to think about it"

This means you've gotten through everything else. You're now talking to someone who has the power to decide. Help them make the decision.

- "Time doesn't give you more information — I am the information. What specifically do you want to think through? Let's do it now."
- Three questions to walk through together:
  1. Do you believe this gets you where you want to go?
  2. Do you trust me to deliver?
  3. Do you think it'll work for you specifically?
- If all three are yes: "Then the only question is whether you have access to the money. Do you?"
- "Every year of almost is still almost. At what point do we stop planning to start?"

## Follow-Up After the Call

Most deals don't close on the first conversation. Most people follow up once, hear nothing, and assume it's dead. It's usually not.

**The sequence:**

| When | What to say |
|---|---|
| Same day | Send a recap of what you talked about + one line on what happens next |
| Day 3 | Add something — a result, a relevant example, a question they didn't finish answering |
| Day 7 | Short check-in. "Still thinking it through, or did something come up?" |
| Day 14 | One more. Bring something new — a client win, a relevant observation |
| Day 30 | Last one. Close the loop, leave the door open |

**The frame:** You're not chasing. You're staying available for someone who hasn't decided yet. Big difference in how it feels when you write it.

**If they go quiet:**
> "No worries if timing's off — I just wanted to make sure you had everything you needed to make a good call either way."

That line does two things: removes pressure, and re-opens the door without begging.

**The rule:** Each follow-up adds something. Never "just checking in" — that's noise. Bring a reason to reply.

## The Diagnostic Close — For Build Conversations

Instead of pitching your service, sell the outcome they described.

1. Ask where they are now
2. Ask where they want to be
3. You provide the path — and price it to the goal

**Example:**
> "You said you're spending 10 hours a week on this manually. If I get that to under 1 hour — what's that worth to you?"

Now the price came from their answer, not from you. Much easier to say yes to.

## The Bone-Simple Rules

- **Ask for the sale.** If you don't ask, you don't get it.
- **Resolve the concern, then ask again.** You can do this as many times as needed. It's not pushy — it's caring.
- **The person who cares more about the prospect wins the sale.** If they care more about themselves than you care about helping them, they walk.
- **You're not selling them.** You're helping them make a decision they already want to make but are afraid to.
- **The reason they're not buying is usually the reason they need it most.**
