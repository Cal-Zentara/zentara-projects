The One Thing Everything Points To
Authenticity beats polish every time in a local race. A shaky phone video of Byron at a District 5 event will outperform a produced campaign ad. Voters need to know him before they'll care about his positions. Social media's job right now is trust-building — not persuasion. Once people feel like they know Byron and believe he cares, they'll listen to policy. Skip that step, and policy posts fall flat.
1
Instagram Strategy
  • Reels are the only free growth tool. Instagram pushes Reels to non-followers. This is the only way to reach people who don't already know Byron. Aim for 2–3 per week — this is the highest priority on IG.
  • Stories every day. No production needed. Behind-the-scenes clips, quick updates, polls. Keeps existing followers warm without burning feed content.
  • Best times to post: Wednesday 12pm and 6pm, Thursday 9am. Post within those windows for maximum reach.
  • Local hashtags only: #IrvineCA #IrvineD5 #IrvineCouncil + neighborhood tags like #TurtleRock #Woodbridge. Use what people already search — don't invent new ones.
  • Growth from zero is realistic. Consistent posting + engaging on Irvine community accounts = 500–1,000 real local followers in 4–6 weeks. Target engaged locals, not vanity numbers.
  • Track saves, not likes. Saves mean people want to come back to the post. That is the strongest signal a post is working. Likes are passive — saves mean interest.
  • Engage on local content. Comment on posts from Irvine city council, local news, community organizations. Builds visibility without being salesy.
2
Facebook Strategy
  • Groups are the single biggest move on Facebook. Find and join 15–30 Irvine neighborhood and community groups. Spend 2–3 weeks just answering questions and being helpful before mentioning the campaign. Show up as a neighbor first.
  • Post as a person, not a candidate. Ask questions: "What's the biggest issue on your street right now?" Comment-generating posts get 3x more organic reach than announcements.
  • Facebook Events for every appearance. Create an event for every fundraiser and community showing. Events get natural reach in local feeds — ads don't. This is the most impactful move on Facebook.
  • Boost top posts for $5–10 once they prove themselves organically. Spend money on what's already working, not on guesses. Small boosts on proven content outperform big ad spend.
  • Post max 2x/week on the feed. More than that burns out the audience. Consistency over volume.
  • Go Live once a week. 15–20 minute Q&A, answer real questions live. Highest engagement format on Facebook and makes Byron feel accessible and real.
  • Target age 55+ when boosting. Older voters are on Facebook daily and they actually show up to the polls.
  • Respond to every comment within 2 hours. Shows engagement and that Byron is actually listening — not just broadcasting.

What Every Post Should Be About
Content Type How Much What It Looks Like
Byron's Story 30% Why he's running, what he's seen in D5, personal moments that connect
Issues + Solutions 40% One local problem per post, Byron's specific answer — never vague
Community Presence 20% Him at events, with residents, door-knocking — proof he's showing up
Calls to Action 10% Donate, RSVP, share — but only after earning it. Never lead with asks.
The 80/20 Rule
80% valuable content (story, community, education, issues). 20% asks (donate, come to the fundraiser, share this). If every post is an ask, people tune out fast.
4
Video — No Budget Needed
Length
30–44 seconds is the sweet spot for Reels. Anything over 60 seconds and engagement drops sharply. Short, land the point, done.
Format That Wins
Talking head — Byron direct to camera. People vote for people, not B-roll footage. The more human, the better it performs.
Production
Phone is fine. Only buy worth making: a $15–30 stabilizer. Shoot in natural morning or afternoon light. Quiet room. Bad audio kills a video; shaky footage doesn't.
Hooks That Work
Lead with a local pain point in the first 2 seconds. "Did you know District 5 has..." beats "Hi, I'm Byron and I want to talk about..." — never open with your name.
Shooting Tip
Shoot 3–5 takes, pick the one with the best eye contact and fewest stumbles. Never expect a one-take to work. Practice once, then record.
What to Avoid
No trending music — it dates the video and distracts from the message. No over-editing. No B-roll-heavy cuts that hide Byron's face. People need to see him.

What Kills New Campaigns — Avoid These
No Clear Message
Every post needs a reason to exist. If someone reads it and can't say what Byron stands for, the post failed. Every post supports one core story.
Inconsistent Schedule
Missing a week kills momentum and the algorithm buries the account. Voters forget about you. 3 posts a week forever beats 10 posts one week and nothing for two weeks.
Counting Followers
500 followers who comment and share beats 5,000 ghost followers every time. Engagement is the only number that matters. Focus on real local people, not vanity metrics.
Copy-Paste Across Platforms
Same post, same caption on IG and Facebook doesn't work. Different audiences, different formats. Same message — but adapted for how each platform actually works.
Sounding Like a Politician
Corporate phrases, vague promises, zero personality. "Fighting for our families" means nothing. "Here's why Irvine's water rates went up and what I'd change" is what works.
Leading With Asks
Don't ask for donations or volunteers before building relationship. Earn the ask by giving value first. If every post is a pitch, people tune out fast.

What Actually Wins — Real Examples
AOC — 2018 Congressional Campaign
$10,000 total campaign video budget. All volunteers and family. Candidate wrote her own script. Got 300,000 views in one day. Won because it felt real — not produced. The low budget forced the authenticity that made it work.
Zohran Mamdani — NYC Mayoral Race
Shot videos at food trucks and bodegas, talking to real people about how city policies affected their lives. No studio, no scripts. Organic conversations that viewers could feel. Voters saw themselves in the content — that's what drove shares.
iFOCUS Local Candidate — 7-Month Case Study
Posted 4–5 times per week across platforms for 7 straight months. Results: 36,000 Facebook engagements, 461 new page likes, 7,600 Twitter engagements. Numbers aren't massive — but the candidate won. Consistency over 7 months beat everything else.
The Pattern Across All Three
Unknown candidates don't lose on policy. They lose because voters never got to know them. Social media's only job in the first few months is making Byron feel like a real person voters can trust — not selling them on positions.
