## LEAD MAGNET — Hormozi Framework (April 19, 2026)

Source: Alex Hormozi, acquisition.com — portfolio companies did $250M last year. This is from his book "$100M Leads" (page 31).

---

### The Core Problem

Most businesses send traffic to their website and immediately ask people to buy or book a call. Problem: most visitors aren't ready to buy yet. They leave and never come back. You're losing almost all your traffic before they ever give you a chance.

The fix isn't more traffic. It's converting a higher percentage of the traffic you already have. That's why this costs nothing extra — it drops straight to your bottom line.

---

### What a Lead Magnet Is

A mini offer that's a complete solution to a narrow problem. Free or very low cost. It solves one specific problem, and once it's solved, it reveals a bigger problem — which your paid offer fixes.

**The math:**
- 100 people, ask them to buy → ~1 buys
- 100 people, give free thing first, then ask → 3–5x more buyers
- Same traffic. Same ad spend. Just more conversions.

**Why it works:** When someone pays with time, they're more likely to pay with money later. You're lowering the first barrier so the second one is easier to clear.

### Hormozi's Personal Story

April 2016 — he paid $25k to be in a mastermind. Everyone told him to do a webinar. He did it. It didn't work (he didn't have the skill yet).

He saw an ad scrolling his feed: "Free case study — how I spent $1 and made $120,000 in a weekend." He opted in, watched a screen recording of how the guy did it. Thought: I could do that.

He swapped his webinar for: "Free case study — how we added 213 members to a gym and $112,000 in San Diego."

Next morning his partner said: "What did you do? My calendar's full."

**His lesson:** A webinar signals "sales pitch" to a sophisticated audience. They don't opt in. But "hey, here's a screen recording, consume it on your own time" — people are way more willing to exchange contact info for that. Same pitch, different wrapper. Completely different result.

### The 3 Types of Lead Magnets

#### Type 1 — Reveal a Problem (Assessment)

Show them something is wrong — either something they didn't know existed, or something they knew about but didn't understand how bad it actually is. The moment they find out, you've created deprivation.

**Best for:** Problems that get worse while waiting. The urgency is built in — every second they wait, they're losing more.

Examples:
- Free website speed test → "your site loads in 9 seconds. For every second of load time, you lose 3% of conversions. That's 18% of your business you're leaving on the table right now."
- Posture analysis → "your posture's bad and it's only getting worse"
- Termite inspection → "they're already active and the damage compounds"
- Financial audit → "you're behind on taxes and every month it gets worse"

**Hormozi's highest converting ad ever:** "Four reasons why you'll never have a million-dollar agency." He was selling to small SMB lead gen agencies. He listed out 4 reasons people stay stuck. Most readers had all 4. They assumed: if he knows exactly what's wrong, he can fix it.

**The strategy behind this:** Be incredibly specific on the negatives. Be vague on the positives. You market more honestly, set realistic expectations, and the specificity of the problems does the selling for you. If you nail someone's exact situation and say "I can help with that" — they believe you.

Show them:
1. Here's your problem
2. Here's how bad it is
3. Here's what it could look like solved
4. Here's the gap (the delta)

The deprivation increases the longer they wait. That's urgency without lying.

---

#### Type 2 — Free Trial (Taste Test)

Classic "try before you buy." Let them experience the real product or service, then take it away. The removal is what creates the deprivation that gets them to pay.

You're not giving them the whole thing — you're giving them enough that they get used to having it, so they can't imagine losing it.

**Gym Launch example:** They'd sell gyms their monetization system (how to make the gym more profitable). Along with that: "We'll give you agency services — lead generation — free for 4 months." After 4 months of leads just showing up, they'd say "Hey, that was the trial. Want to keep it going?" Almost everyone said yes. They'd been living with the leads. They couldn't go back.

**What you limit in a free trial:**
- Time (30-day trial)
- Number of uses (10 hits)
- Quantity (only the first session)
- Some combination

The more complete the trial feels, the more deprivation when it's removed. That's the goal.

---

#### Type 3 — One Step of Many

Solve the first step in a multi-step process. They get real value from that step, but now they have a new problem — the next step — which only your paid offer solves.

**Best for:** Complex products or services where results require multiple phases.

Examples:
- Laser hair removal — takes 6–8 sessions to actually work. Give the first one free. Now they need 5–7 more.
- Multiple coats of paint — sell the first coat. They'll need the rest.
- Course — give the first 2 videos free. Now they want the rest.
- Ashley the personal stylist (Hormozi's example): First step is figuring out your colors. Once you have colors, you still need to figure out tops/bottoms, formal/informal, etc. Infinite follow-on problems.

The key: the free step must be genuinely useful on its own. But it makes the deprivation for the next steps obvious.

### 4 Ways to Deliver a Lead Magnet

#### 1. Software / Tool
Something interactive that does a job for them or spits out an answer. Can be used to fulfill any of the 3 types.

Examples:
- Spreadsheet that calculates something
- Assessment quiz → categorizes them into one of 4 buckets
- Template (dynamic, not static)
- Actual software

**Neil Patel's example:** Enter your URL, get a site assessment. Collects info on the backend while delivering value up front.

**Hormozi's scaling road map:** You go through the tool, it assesses what stage of business you're at and what problems you're dealing with right now, gives you exactly how to solve them. Then offers a call where his team looks at your actual business.

---

#### 2. Information
Teach them something valuable. Costs nothing to produce, infinitely scalable, zero operational drag once it's created.

Examples:
- Mini course
- Guide / PDF
- Expert interview
- Static templates
- Case study (screen recording, walk-through)

**Why it works:** Information is cheap to create but can be enormously valuable to the right person. The key is making it specific — not generic "tips" but actual answers to a real question your ideal client has.

---

#### 3. Services (Do Work for Free)
Most slept-on option. Actually do something for them. But only for qualified leads — this is critical.

**The math:**
- Hard cost: $25/hour of labor
- What you could charge: $250+
- Conversion rate: 1 out of 4 who get it convert to a paid client
- Customer acquisition cost: $25 × 4 = $100
- Would you pay $100 to get a customer? Probably yes, if your offer is worth more.

**Qualifying is mandatory.** Don't do free work for broke people who can't make decisions and don't really need what you sell. Only give this to people who have budget, authority, need, and timing. Add a qualifier upfront (dropdown, form question, quick call) and redirect unqualified leads to something else.

Types of free services:
- Free audit with some implementation included
- Same-day service delivery
- Done-for-you component as a taste

**Hormozi's first business:** He trained people for free for a full year. Called it the "Free Training Project." Goal was testimonials. After a year, he told them: "I have too much demand, do you want to pay me now?" Almost all of them said yes. None were freebie seekers — they just needed the proof first.

---

#### 4. Physical Products
Books, samples, branded items. Can be combined with information (book = info + physical) or used purely as a qualifier.

**Hormozi's books** (like $100M Leads itself) are lead magnets. After reading, you want to know more → book a call → become a portfolio company candidate.

**CEO t-shirt example at a conference:** Give away a shirt that says CEO, but only to people who prove they're actually a CEO. $3–4 cost per shirt. 1 out of 20 converts to a paid client. That's $80 per CEO customer. Works great if your offer is worth more than $80 to land a CEO client.

The qualifier built into the giveaway does the targeting for you.

### Naming Your Lead Magnet (Most Slept-On Part)

How you name it will determine your opt-in rate more than anything else — including what's actually inside it.

You can change how many people want your lead magnet by 2x, 5x, even 10x simply by changing the headline. The contents barely matter if nobody opts in to see them.

**How to test it:**
- Run small ad tests with different headlines (best method if you have budget)
- Post in your stories: "Help me out — which sounds better to you?" (filter: only people matching your avatar)
- Text people directly and ask A or B
- Make a post: "Comment A or B"

Hormozi split tested 6 different headlines for "$100M Leads" — also tested the cover image and sub-headline. He spent 2 days testing the name of a book he spent 2 years writing, because the name was going to influence how many people picked it up more than anything else (short term).

**Naming formulas:**

1. **Number + outcome + timeframe**
   → "3 emails that turn cold leads into clients in 24 hours"

2. **How to X without Y (even if Z)**
   → "How to build a funnel without hiring a copywriter, even if you've never done it before"
   → How to [good thing] without [bad thing / fear] even if [greatest insecurity]

3. **Adjective + type + result**
   → "The lazy funnel template that converts like crazy"

4. **X mistakes**
   → "4 mistakes that are keeping your business under a million dollars a year"
   → "5 ad copy mistakes that are killing your conversion rate"
   → "3 mistakes you're probably making with [thing]"

**The gym example:** He ran a 6-week deadlift/squat/hip thrust class. His audience was women.
- "6-Week Deadlift and Squat Seminar" → didn't convert
- "Big Booty Boot Camp" → converted

Same workouts. Totally different result. You don't advertise the vehicle. You advertise the destination.

### The Key Rule — Don't Give Away Your Core Offer

You must NOT solve the problem your paid offer solves with the free thing.

**Why:** If someone comes in hungry and you feed them a full meal, they're not hungry anymore. Asking "want another meal?" right after gets a no — not because your food was bad, but because they're not deprived.

**The restaurant analogy:** Entree fills the hunger. Dessert fills a different need. At the right moment, after the entree, the dessert craving kicks in. That's when you sell it — and make it more expensive than the entree.

**The water analogy:** If someone hasn't had water in a while, sell them water. The moment after they drink it is the worst time to sell water — they're not thirsty. But maybe now they're hungry. Sell food.

**You want to sell at the point of greatest deprivation.**

The lead magnet solves one thing, which triggers a new deprivation, which your paid offer resolves.

### Handling Objections

**"I don't want freebie seekers."**
Then qualify them. Add a dropdown or a qualifier before they access the free thing. Redirect unqualified leads to a different track. Only give the good stuff to people who match your ideal customer profile. You don't have to give it to everyone.

**"I don't want to give away too much value."**
You're not giving away your core thing. You're giving away something adjacent that makes them need your core thing more. If they get full, they don't want more of the same. Give them something that makes them hungry for the main course.

### After They Consume It — Ask for the Sale

Most people build a great lead magnet and forget to actually ask people to buy. This is where the money is.

**CTA formula:**
1. **Clear, not clever** — say exactly what you want them to do. No ambiguity.
2. **Exact next action** — "click here," "book a call," "reply to this email." One action.
3. **Reason to do it now** — scarcity, urgency, or any reason at all

**The research on reasons:** In a study, people were cutting in line at a university copy machine. "Can I cut?" → No. "Can I cut because I'm late for class?" → Yes. "Can I cut because I have a dog?" → Still yes. The reason didn't even have to make sense. It just had to exist. Any reason is better than no reason.

Examples of weak reasons that still work:
- "It's my one-year anniversary in business, so I'm running this promotion"
- "My daughter just lost her first tooth — here's a little celebration offer"
- "It's National Dog Day — if you have a dog you should especially do this"

Use real scarcity when you have it. Service businesses almost always do — you have limited capacity. If 4 more clients fills you up, say so. People don't know how big or small your business is. Use your actual constraint.

If you're a big business with lots of capacity: use cohorts. "We only onboard X people per week. If you want in this Friday's group, do it now."

**Ask often. Give value between every ask.** The formula is: value → ask → value → ask. Never just ask. Never just give. The more value you deliver per minute, the more willing people are to hear your pitch.

---

### The Full System — Summary

1. **Give value first** — reveal a problem / free trial / one step of many
2. **Deliver it via** — software, information, services, physical, or any combo
3. **Name it to convert** — test the headline. Not the contents. The packaging.
4. **Embed CTAs** — inside the lead magnet, before it, and after it. Ask clearly, ask with a reason, ask often.
