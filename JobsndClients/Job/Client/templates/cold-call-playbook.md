# Cold-call playbook (web pitch to local shops)

Read this before dialing. The goal of the call is NOT to sell a website. It's to get one small yes: **"Can I text you an example?"** Everything else is bonus.

---

## Your positioning (how to frame yourself)

**Say this (to barbershops):** "I build websites for barbershops — one flat price, live within a week, gets you found on Google."

**Say this (to nail salons):** "I build websites for nail salons — one flat price, live within a week, gets you found on Google."

**Rule: always name THEIR trade.** Not "shops," not "local businesses," not "small businesses." The word "barbershops" coming out of your mouth is what makes Danny think *this guy knows my world.* If you're calling a taqueria next month, the line becomes "I build websites for restaurants."

**Never say:** "freelance web designer," "web development services," or anything that sounds like an agency menu. That invites price-shopping and "send me a quote" brush-offs.

Why this framing works:

- **You're a specialist, not a generalist.** "I build sites for barbershops" beats "I build websites" — and it beats "I build sites for Anaheim shops" too. Trade first, town second. People trust someone who knows their world.
- **You're local.** "I'm here in Anaheim" — you can stop by, they can call you. An agency can't say that.
- **You sell the outcome, not the thing.** They don't want a website; they want more booked chairs. Every sentence should connect to their money.
- **Flat price, fast delivery.** "One price, live by Friday" kills the two fears: cost spiraling and the project dragging.

---

## The pitch (30 seconds, then shut up)

1. **Who you are + why them:** "Hi, is this [Danny]? I'm Cal, I'm local here in Anaheim — I build websites for barbershops."
2. **Their specific gap:** "I went looking for [shop]'s website and couldn't find one — when someone googles 'barber near me,' shops with a site show up first and you don't."
3. **Outcome, not features:** "I build clean one-pagers — your work, hours, and a book-now button — so those searches land in your chair instead of someone else's."
4. **One small ask:** "Can I text you an example to look at? Takes ten seconds."

Then **stop talking.** Silence after the ask is your friend. Whoever talks next, loses leverage.

---

## What to listen for (qualifying on the fly)

**Good signs — lean in:**

- You're talking to the owner, and they stay on the line.
- "Yeah, we've been meaning to do that…" — they already know it's a gap. Easiest sale there is.
- "My nephew was supposed to build one" — translation: nobody's doing it. "If it hasn't happened yet, that's the answer. I'll have it live by Friday."
- Busy shop noise (clippers, chatter) — busy means money.
- They ask a question back — any question. Curiosity = interest.

**Bad signs — wrap up politely and move to the next call:**

- "All our business is walk-ins, we don't need the internet" said with zero hesitation. One counter, then let go.
- Instant haggling before you've even quoted. Price-shoppers make nightmare clients.
- "Send me info" with no engagement — that's a soft no. Say "Sure — what's the best number to text?" If they won't give it, it's a no.

**If it's not the owner:** don't pitch. Get two things: the owner's name and when they're in. "No worries — who handles that stuff, and when do they usually come in?" Log it, call back.

---

## Questions to ask (pick 2–3, don't interrogate)

1. **"Where do most of your new customers find you right now?"** — Opens the conversation, gets them talking about their business. Whatever they answer, the follow-up is "and where do the ones who *search* end up?"
2. **"How do people book with you — walk-in, call, Booksy?"** — Tells you what the site needs to link to, and shows you're thinking about their workflow, not selling a template.
3. **"Have you ever had a website before? What happened?"** — Reveals the real objection upfront (got burned, too expensive, nephew flaked).
4. **"If you had five more new faces a week, could you handle them?"** — Gets them picturing the outcome. Capacity = appetite.

---

## Objections and answers

| They say | You say |
|---|---|
| "Word of mouth is enough for us" | "That's exactly why it'll work — you're already the shop people recommend. This just catches the people who *search* instead of ask." |
| "I've got Booksy / Instagram" | "Booksy ranks Booksy, not you. And Google barely shows Instagram. Those are your back office — the website is the front door." |
| "Websites are expensive" | "Mine's one flat price, one time. Less than what one chair makes on a Saturday, and it works for you every day after." |
| "My cousin/nephew is gonna do it" | "How long's that been the plan? [pause] I'll have it live by Friday, and your cousin can take the credit." |
| "I'm busy, not now" | "Totally get it — I'll just text you the example. Ten seconds to look, no follow-up if you hate it. What's the best number?" |

---

## Pricing (know it, don't lead with it)

- **One-page site: $500–$1,000 flat.** 50% deposit to start, rest when it's live. Don't quote until they ask or until they're clearly interested.
- If they ask early: give the range with zero hesitation. Hesitation reads as negotiable.
- **The upsell (after the yes, not during the pitch):** "Want me to keep it updated and handle your Google listing for $75/month?" — that's recurring money.
- For the two shops with no Instagram (Barbers II, OC Barber): once the site lands, offer social management as a follow-up conversation, not in call #1. One thing per call.

Update `leads.md`: answered? owner reached? what they said? next step (text example / call back Tuesday / dead). A call you don't log is a lead you'll lose.
