## 🧭 Start Here — The Roadmap (how it all fits together)

These are the 10 things that take a stranger from never hearing of UNIK to buying from us. They go in order. Skip the early ones and you're asking a parent to buy before they trust you. Right now our ads are strong on the *last* few (product, price, USP) and skip the early ones (warming people up, removing fear, proof). That's the leak this roadmap closes.

### 1. The Marketing Mix — the 4 things every decision fits into (#1)
- **Product** — school uniforms built to last the whole year.
- **Price** — most polos and blouses are **$12.99** (the loud hook). Whole catalog runs $9.99 (fleece sweats) to $33.99 (winter coats); the core uniform is all under $20. Lead with price on the under-$15 staples; on pricier items (jackets, coats) lead with quality.
- **Place** (source: Cal, June 2 2026) — **Wholesale (unikla.com) is their core business — what they do best.** That's why the @unikusa bio talks to resellers. **Retail (direct to parents) is the growth lane our content targets.** Within retail today: Amazon = most sales → Shein = some → own webpage (unikusa.com) = very little. **CTA goal: drive to the WEBPAGE.** Even though Amazon converts better right now, the webpage is the one channel they fully own (better margins, keep the customer, no marketplace fees) — growing it is the point. Physical HQ/store: 501 E Pico Blvd, Los Angeles, CA 90015.
- **Promotion** — IG + FB content. This is our weakest leg and the whole focus of this roadmap. **Live account: @unikusa (481 followers, 178 posts).** ⚠️ Right now the bio and content speak to *resellers* ("Become a Unik reseller today"), not to parents. Our roadmap targets LA moms — so either the parent content needs its own positioning on this account, or a parent-facing handle. Flag for Cal before posting.
All four have to match. A quality product needs consistent messaging behind it.

### 2. Who we're talking to (#2 — full detail in "The Audience" below)
**Who we're talking to = LA moms.** Period. Defined by who SHE is, not by any sales channel: a busy LA mom, runs the household, makes the back-to-school call, buying for her kids. Latina moms are the core (full detail in "The Audience" below). Every post speaks to that one mom, not "everyone."
**Positioning:** priced like Cat & Jack, built like Lands' End — premium quality at an affordable price. (Quality first, the price is the pleasant surprise. Never call it "cheap" — that's the opposite of what the reviews prove.)
**Don't confuse the audience with the sales channel:** WHO we talk to = moms. WHERE money currently comes from = Amazon/Shein (a separate fact, see "Place" in #1). We talk to moms regardless of where they end up buying.
**The content job:** build the UNIK name with moms and drive them to the **webpage** to buy direct — growing the channel the cousins own. Social builds the brand AND the destination.

### 3. The one thing we stand for (#4 Brand Building)
**The brand promise: "The LA family that's kept kids in uniforms that actually last — for 20 years."**

All three pieces stack in one order so they stick — never compete:
- **Headline → Identity: the 20-year LA family.** Lead with this. It's the ONLY thing competitors can't copy — Cat & Jack can cut price, Lands' End can claim quality, but nobody else is *this LA family doing it for 20 years.* It's ours alone.
- **Backup → Proof: it lasts the year.** The reviews prove the family knows what they're doing ("didn't end the year with holes in the knees").
- **Kicker → Surprise: and it's under $15.** Price lands last, as the pleasant surprise — never the lead.

Repeat this until parents say it back without thinking. Brand is the feeling they get when they see the name, not the logo.

### 4. The Funnel — the spine of everything (#8)
The journey from never-heard-of-us to buying. Don't propose on the first date.
- **Top (Awareness)** — they don't know us. Give them helpful + relatable content (back-to-school tips, checklists, Chaos POV, Deal Alert hooks).
- **Middle (Consideration)** — they know us. Build trust (real reviews, customer photos, the 20-years story, Proof pillar).
- **Bottom (Conversion)** — they're ready. Hit them with the USP, the price, and a clear CTA (Hero Films, deal posts with "comment GET LINK").

**Our 4 pillars mapped onto the funnel:**
| Pillar | Funnel stage |
|---|---|
| Chaos POV | Top (awareness/relatability) |
| Deal Alerts | Top hook → Bottom close |
| Proof | Middle (trust) |
| Hero Films | Bottom (brand + conversion) |

### 5. What's really going on in the parent's head (#3 Consumer Psychology)
People decide with emotion, then justify with logic. We don't have to guess UNIK's customers' feelings — the real Amazon reviews (`review-gold.md`, ~80% positive) tell us. Lead with the STRENGTH the reviews prove, not a defensive angle:
- **Durability ("will it last the year?") — already WON.** Reviews crush this: "the only ones that didn't end the year with holes in the knees," "the pants did not rip, not even a snag," "bought some in 2021 and still looks good in 2022." This is the loudest, most-repeated theme. Make it the spine of trust content.
- **Loyalty / trust ("do other parents stick with it?") — proven.** "I get it every year." "My son lives in these." Repeat-buying is the emotional proof — pair it with the 20-years family story.
- **Relief ("finally, affordable AND it lasts").** The combo is the magic: a low price usually means flimsy; UNIK is affordable AND survives the whole year. That's the feeling to sell.
- *(Sizing confusion / returns shows up in reviews but it's a minor gripe with no real volume behind it — do NOT build content around it. Lead with strength.)*
Stop selling the uniform. Sell the relief of "affordable AND it lasts." Never use the word "cheap" — it signals flimsy, the opposite of the truth. Real review quotes beat any "Shop Now."

### 6. The USP — why buy from UNIK, not Walmart/Amazon (#6)
**"The LA family that's kept kids in uniforms that actually last — for 20 years."** (Same as the brand promise — the USP and the promise are one thing here.) Why it holds up:
- **Specific** — not "good quality" (everyone says that). It's *this family, 20 years, uniforms that survive the year.*
- **True + provable** — the reviews prove the durability; the 20 years is real history.
- **Matters to mom** — she's tired of rebuying ripped uniforms, and tired of faceless megastores.
Walmart's a machine. Amazon's a search box. UNIK is real LA people who've earned it. That's the edge nobody can copy.

### 7. How to write any post — AIDA (#7)
The 4 steps every post moves through, in order. Family voice, no dashes (copy rule):
- **Attention** (stop the scroll): "Every August, the same panic. Why are uniforms this expensive?"
- **Interest** (now she cares): "We're an LA family that's been making them for 20 years. The kind that actually make it to summer."
- **Desire** (now she wants it): "Some of our moms have been with us since their first kid. One said it best: the only ones that didn't end the year with holes in the knees." *(carry desire with a real review quote, not a claim)*
- **Action** (tell her exactly what to do): "Come see them at unikusa.com." *(CTA goes to the webpage, always; warm, not "shop now")*
Earn the "shop now," don't skip straight to it. **Family is the spine** (the un-copyable lead), durability is the proof, price is the kicker. Loud deal-alert hooks can stop the scroll up top, but every one still lands on the family as the reason to trust.

### 8. The 3 ways to get attention — POEM (#9)
- **Paid** — FB/IG ads. Fast but costs money. Later, when budget's ready.
- **Owned** — our IG, FB, and the webpage. Free but slow. **The focus right now.**
- **Earned** — parents tagging, reviewing, sharing. The most powerful. Ask every single customer for a tag or review (and mine `review-gold.md` for the ones we already have).
One mom posting "got my kid's polo, thirteen bucks and it lasted the whole year" beats any ad.
**Where we are right now (June 2026):** ZERO paid spend — no Meta ads, no TikTok ads. Pure organic: produce content, post it, watch what lands. Paid only comes after something proves it works organically.

### 9. The weekly rhythm + how we know it's working (#10)
- **Post 3x/week:** 1 helpful + 1 trust + 1 selling. Every week.
- **The rhythm only works with a SYSTEM (Cal's call, June 2):** don't create post-by-post. Batch a whole week (or month) at once from the post idea bank below, using repeatable templates. Make in bulk, schedule, walk away. Building this system is the unlock that makes 3x/week realistic instead of a scramble. (Candidate for a content tool/skill later.)
- **Track posts:** which get saves, shares, DMs? Do more of those.
- **Track customers:** ask every buyer "How'd you hear about us?"
- **The goal isn't viral.** It's a few hundred LA moms who know and trust the name and buy direct. That's it.

### 10. How we sound (#5 Brand Voice — full detail in "The Voice" section below)
The family speaking, first person. Funny to grab attention, warm to earn trust. Never lead with price. If it sounds like an ad, rewrite it.

### Post idea bank — never stare at a blank screen
- **Helpful (top):** ready-to-use, SOURCED facts live in `unik-helpful-content-bank.md` — how-many-uniforms, keep-whites-white, LA dress-code rules, back-to-school budgeting, and special-needs angles (sensory/husky/active). Pull a fact, write a post.
- **Trust (middle):** real Amazon review quotes (**10 ready-to-use lines in `review-gold.md`** — e.g. "The only ones that didn't end the year with holes in the knees"), "every kid in this photo wears UNIK," pickup-line shots, the 20-year family story, real DMs.
- **Selling (bottom):** $12.99 polo vs $32.95 Lands' End comparison, the GU07 skirt 4-color showcase, the finished video ads, bundle math ("dress 1 kid for the year for $X"). Every CTA → the webpage.

## The Voice

Real. Bilingual. Relatable. Funny. Built from scratch — UNIK has no existing consumer voice, so we're creating it for the school uniform line.

**Who's speaking = the family.** Not a faceless brand, not a hired creator — the actual LA family who've run UNIK for 20 years. First person ("we," "our family," "we've been dressing LA kids since..."). This is the single biggest voice lever: "we got you" from a faceless brand is an ad; "we got you" from the family who's done it 20 years is real. When in doubt, write it the way one of the cousins would actually say it to a mom in the store.

Core feeling: a parent in LA who knows how to stretch a dollar and isn't embarrassed about it.

**Yes:**
- "POV: it's 7am and the uniform is still in the dryer"
- "Same polos two kids later. Still no holes. Make it make sense."
- "Tell me you have 3 kids in school without telling me"
- "I'm not crying you're crying — first day of kinder fit check"
- "The LA family your tía's been buying uniforms from for 20 years"

**No:**
- "At UNIK, we believe quality matters"
- "Designed for the modern family"
- "Our premium fabrics ensure comfort and durability"
- Any sentence that could come from a Lands' End catalog

**Tone dial:** Lean relatable and funny. Bilingual when it fits naturally — not forced. Parents (moms and dads) should both feel seen. Latina parents are the core but the voice should land for any LA parent watching.

**When funny vs. when warm (so they don't clash):**
- **Funny/relatable** → the everyday "top of funnel" posts (POV chaos, the 7am dryer panic, deal alerts). This is the scroll-stopper voice.
- **Warm/heartfelt** → the trust + family posts (the 20-year story, real review quotes, "I get it every year"). Drop the jokes here — sincerity is the point.
- Same brand, two settings: funny gets the attention, warm earns the trust. Never force a joke onto the family story.

**Price language:** Never lead a line with the price. Price is the kicker, not the hook (see the brand promise). And get the number right — polos are **$12.99**, not $9.99.

**Note:** This voice is being built for the school uniform line specifically. UNIK's wholesale side doesn't need this — this is for consumer-facing IG and FB content only.

## The 4 Content Pillars

Every piece of content fits one of these. Mix the ratio based on what's converting.

### 1. Deal Alerts (the workhorse)

Reference: @vivacristina — 2M views on a $3 zoo deal post.

Format:
- Side-by-side: UNIK price vs competitor price
- "$12.99 vs $32.95" big text overlay
- Voice: "Why are we paying $32 for the same polo?"
- CTA: link in bio, comment GET LINK

This is what we lead with. Cheapest format to produce, highest viral ceiling.

### 2. Chaos POV (the relatability play)

Format:
- POV setup: "POV: school starts in 3 days and you haven't bought a single uniform"
- Mom on camera, fast cuts, dramatic music
- Solution = UNIK in 2 clicks

Voice fits perfectly here. This is where bilingual code-switching lives.

### 3. Hero Films (the brand play)

Format:
- Longer, more cinematic
- Kids in uniform, real LA settings
- Music-driven, light VO
- Built to make UNIK feel premium even at the price

Use sparingly. 1 per month max. These are credibility builders, not converters. The v5 hallway ad is the closest we've gotten so far (7/10).

### 4. Proof (the trust play)

Format:
- Real customer photos / DMs / reviews (when we have them)
- School pickup line shots
- "Every kid in this photo is wearing UNIK"
- UGC style, low production

Phase 2 mostly — we don't have enough real customer content yet. Build slowly.

## Production Rules (UNIK Specific)

- Default video length: 15 seconds for IG/FB feed, 30 for stories
- Aspect ratio: 9:16 for reels and stories, 1:1 for feed
- Music: lean bilingual, lean Latin (reggaeton, cumbia, hyperpop)
- Real settings only — LA homes, real schools, parking lots, grocery store. No studio backdrops.
- Faces: stick to the locked reference images already in this folder (boy_face, mom_face)
- AI text on screen is unreliable — keep it short, big, one line max
- Skip Sony Handycam / vintage camera prompts — they hurt realism, not help
- One emotion per shot. Never stack.

## What's Failed (Don't Repeat)

- Wholesome family brand voice — Cal killed it, doesn't fit the audience
- Multi-action shots (drip check + smirk + palm tap) — kids end up looking AI
- "Cinematic" "premium" "filmic" buzzwords in prompts — they flatten the result
- Polished logo end cards — kills the chaos energy we're going for
- French Toast as a benchmark — not the right competitor
- Leaning on creator seeding before we have an audience
